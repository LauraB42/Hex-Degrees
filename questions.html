Riemann hypothesis

The Riemann hypothesis is a conjecture in mathematics that all non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function have a real part equal to 1/2. This hypothesis is significant because it implies results about the distribution of prime numbers and has profound implications across various mathematical domains. It remains one of the most important unsolved problems in pure mathematics and is one of the Millennium Prize Problems offered a million dollars for a solution. The hypothesis has connections to number theory, quantum mechanics, and cryptography, making it a central point of interest in modern mathematics

Find the next biggest prime number

Prime numbers have long fascinated both amateur and professional mathematicians. An integer greater than one is called a prime number if its only divisors are one and itself. The first prime numbers are 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, etc. For example, the number 10 is not prime because it is divisible by 2 and 5. A Mersenne prime is a prime number of the form 2P-1. The first Mersenne primes are 3, 7, 31, and 127 corresponding to P = 2, 3, 5, and 7 respectively. There are now 52 known Mersenne primes.
